Park Picnics

By Becky Hughes and Nikita Richardson

By Becky Hughes and Nikita Richardson

Central Park

You could duck into Central Park every day and discover new pathways and surprises. But the adjoining neighborhoods offer their own attractions: great meals to fuel your explorations. Near the park’s northwest corner, in Manhattan Valley, is the Vietnamese restaurant Saiguette. Across from Lincoln Center is Cafe Fiorello. At the top of Fifth Avenue, on the stretch known as Museum Mile, sits the Africa Center, home of the West African counter-service restaurant Teranga. And on the Upper East Side is the original location of the burger-meister JG Melon.

A lemongrass pork banh mi at Saiguette

935 Columbus Ave, saiguette.com

Chicken Parmesan at Cafe Fiorello

1900 Broadway, cafefiorello.com

A super bowl at Teranga

1280 Fifth Avenue, itsteranga.getbento.com

A burger and fries at JG Melon

1291 Third Avenue, jgmelon-nyc.com

Flushing Meadows Corona Park

The first and most obvious move? Go to the Queens Night Market. (See Priya Krishna’s advice on how to game your visit.) But since the market is open only on Saturdays, and there are six other days of the week to account for, here are two prime options for snacking while staring up at the Unisphere, watching a pickup soccer game or just lying in the grass.

Carnitas tacos from El Jarochito

43-14 111th Street

Empanadas from Empanadas Café

56-27 Van Doren Street, empanadascafe.com

Prospect Park

At 526 acres, Prospect Park has many distinct sections: the leafy, bucolic drive along Prospect Park West, the bustle and chaos of Grand Army Plaza, the commercial and residential strip of Flatbush and Ocean Avenues, and the charming corner where Windsor Terrace unfolds. If you want to wander and don’t have time to throw together an elaborate picnic, here are some to-go treats you can grab on the way in.

The Chicago dog at Dog Day Afternoon

266 L Prospect Park West, dogdaybrooklyn.com

The ExpressYoSelf Smoothie at Hibiscus Brew

546 Flatbush Avenue, hibiscusbrew.co

A pastrami sandwich at Moe’s Pastrami & Burgers

569 Flatbush Avenue, moespastrami.com

A ham-and-cheese sandwich at Winner in the Park

40 West Drive, winner.nyc

Beach Trips

By Nikita Richardson

By Nikita Richardson

Coney Island

The word “formulaic” is normally used as a put-down. But if there’s a formula that’s been perfected, it’s the 2010 revitalization of the 140-year-old local treasure that is Coney Island. The boardwalk is a one-stop shop for eats and sights, and you’ll appreciate that ease on the days when the sun is beating down over the wide concrete expanse of Surf Avenue. Just make sure you eat after you ride the Thunderbolt roller coaster. That 90-degree drop is no joke!

Chili cheese hot dog from Nathan’s Famous

1310 Surf Avenue, nathansfamous.com

Fried clam strips and a frozen piña colada from Paul’s Daughter

1001 Riegelmann Boardwalk

Funnel cake from Deno’s Sweet Shoppe

1025 Riegelmann Boardwalk

Brighton Beach

This is the most neighborhood-y of the neighborhood beaches: The thriving food scene here draws its flavors and energy from the area’s Eastern European and Central Asian communities. Feel free to go maximalist at the large Tashkent Supermarket, an Uzbek grocery store with an incredible buffet of delights like manti, chebureki and pelmeni. Or keep it simple with a gooey khachapuri from the Georgian restaurant Little Georgia, conveniently served to-go in a pizza box.

The buffet at Tashkent Supermarket

713 Brighton Beach Avenue, tashkentsupermarket.com

Potato and cheese khachapuri from Little Georgia

3089 Brighton Sixth Street, little-georgia.business.site

Rockaway Beach

Whether you live and breathe Rockaway Beach in the summer or drop by only on long weekends, the eating is always good. The burger and hot dog joint Ripper’s is a great and easy choice, though the surrounding area is often packed. Here are some equally exciting possibilities, including fish tacos that can’t be denied and proper sit-down options, that are worth enjoying before you head to a more sleepy stretch of sand.

108-10 Rockaway Beach Drive, therockawayhotel.com

City Outings

Alfresco Dining

By Becky Hughes

By Becky Hughes

New Yorkers became accustomed to eating dinner under the plywood roofs of streetside dining structures during the pandemic, but some decidedly more delightful options are available. There’s sidewalk seating, with its distinctly European appeal, the charm of surprising backyard spaces strung with lights, and the unbeatable thrill of dining in the middle of a closed Manhattan street.

Lovely Day

The city’s Open Streets program, which began early in the pandemic, created a summer dinner oasis in NoLIta. Tree-lined Elizabeth Street closes to traffic every evening, and the seating at the Thai cafe Lovely Day sprawls across the asphalt. Take a seat on a red plastic stool or folding chair and enjoy a spicy green curry in the center of a downtown street — a special brand of New York magic.

196 Elizabeth Street, lovelydaynyc.com

Ci Siamo

If you brave the futuristic canyons of Manhattan West, you’ll be rewarded with a great view from Ci Siamo’s newly opened second-floor patio. There’s something distinctly Midtownish about eating fluffy cast-iron focaccia and buffalo butter tagliatelle while basically overlooking a Peloton store, so keep your eyes trained forward at the Empire State Building.

440 West 33rd Street, Suite No. 100, cisiamonyc.com

Panzón

Greenpoint, Brooklyn, might be the unofficial neighborhood of the sneaky, perfect restaurant backyard — and the Mexico City-inspired restaurant Panzón is a great example. From the street, you wouldn’t expect its haven of string lights, hanging plants and cheerful yellow walls. It’s just the right backdrop for eating sticky chamoy tamarind wings and mushroom quesadillas alongside a flight of mezcal.

23 Greenpoint Avenue, panzonbk.com

The Commodore

People-watching may be the best part of dining outdoors, and the Commodore, on one of the busier corners in Williamsburg, Brooklyn, is the best place to do it. You might come for a meal — ideally a hot-chicken breast sandwich with extra pickles and an order of Cadillac nachos — and end up drinking frozen cocktails in a sidewalk lawn chair for six hours.

366 Metropolitan Avenue, thecommodorebrooklyn.com

Williamsburg Rooftop Bars

By Nikita Richardson

By Nikita Richardson

Envision that scene from “The Office” where Michael, Dwight and Andy point imaginary finger guns (and a pair of crossbows) at one another, and you’ve pretty much got a mental image of the arms race among the rooftops of Williamsburg, Brooklyn. The best options sit atop hotels, with Manhattan views and food worth the elevator trip.

Bar Blondeau

What the Wythe Hotel lacks in stature, at only eight floors, it makes up for with the ever-classy Bar Blondeau, a French-leaning rooftop bar that complements the hotel’s French-leaning lobby restaurant, Le Crocodile. The eclectic menu features Japanese fried chicken, boquerones and a $121 seafood tower, but you can always stick to an affordable merguez hot dog with your fizzy Kir royale.

80 Wythe Avenue, Sixth Floor, barblondeau.com

Westlight

The towering William Vale hotel sports this 22nd-floor cocktail bar, where all the small plates are created by Andrew Carmellini’s NoHo Hospitality. Westlight is party-oriented, hosting dance nights in June and beyond. But the food is also serious enough, with influences from Thailand, Vietnam, Mexico, Japan, Peru and other countries.

111 North 12th Street, 22nd Floor, westlightnyc.com

Laser Wolf

Laser Wolf, the popular Philadelphia import atop the Hoxton, is more of a full-service restaurant. You’ll need at least four people to tackle the generous portions of grilled skewers, steaming pita and various salatims (salads and spreads for dipping and sopping) — if you can manage to get a reservation. Your backup plan: Jaffa, a brand-new raw seafood and cocktail bar on the hotel’s second floor, where you can wait for your name to be called.

97 Wythe Avenue, laserwolfbrooklyn.com

Seafood by the Water

By Sara Bonisteel

By Sara Bonisteel

Not everyone gets a summer escape from New York. But for New Yorkers craving a change of scene, there are destinations within city limits, just a bus or ferry ride away. With a breeze and a waterfront view, these restaurants feel more at home on Cape Cod or the Jersey Shore — without the sand in your shoes.

Brooklyn Crab

For a decade now, this three-level seafood situation in Red Hook, Brooklyn, has been offering cold drinks with a sunset view of Lower Manhattan and the Statue of Liberty from the rooftop. It’s a quick walk from the ferry, and a short bus ride from Downtown Brooklyn. Shrimp-topped deviled eggs, seafood rolls and deep-fried lobster tails on a tray of French fries will fill you up as you take in the sights.

24 Reed Street, brooklyncrab.com

Clemente’s Maryland Crab House

It sits off a service road on the Belt Parkway at the outer edge of Sheepshead Bay. But the sound of rush-hour traffic melts into the background as you take your seat on the patio overlooking Gerritsen Beach, ripples of water lapping at the Sea-Doos and WaveRunners parked in the marina. There are blue crabs by the stainless-steel bowlful, doused in Old Bay or served “Clemente’s style” with roasted garlic and oil. You’ll also find Italian pastas, steakhouse fare and, blissfully for parents, a kids’ menu.

3939 Emmons Avenue, clementescrabhouse.com

Johnny’s Reef

City Island, in the Bronx, has long drawn weekend sailors as well as families looking for an affordable seafood feast in the sunshine. (And it’s a perfect stop when visiting nearby Orchard Beach.) Several of the restaurants specialize in a particular style of cafeteria dining, with separate lines for steamed seafood, fried seafood, grill items and cocktails. Johnny’s Reef, at the end of the road on Belden Point, has the most charm, with hand-painted signs and a patio that overlooks the Merchant Marine Academy on Kings Point. Golden fried shrimp, lobster rolls (heavy on the celery and mayo, but at two for $24, who cares about the filler?) and summer vibes abound.

2 City Island Avenue, johnnysreefrestaurant.com

Yankee Stadium

By Pete Wells

By Pete Wells

True, there is plenty to eat inside Yankee Stadium if you get hungry during a game. The Halal Guys fry falafel, Streetbird cooks chicken and waffles, Benihana stuffs sushi burritos with spicy tuna, and you’re never far from a burger or a dog. One of these may take care of your stomach’s needs. But what of your spiritual appetites? When your soul calls out for a true taste of the Bronx, you have to leave the ballpark.

Mofongo Tipico

You can order crunchy scraps of chicken-skin chicharrones, simmering pots of sancocho, plates of stewed Dominican beef and shiny red lengths of longaniza. But the page of the menu devoted to mofongo calls it “por lo que realmente vinimos” (“what we really came for”), and that’s probably right. Each mofongo is a heap of fried and pounded plantain, wide at the bottom and flat on top, like the Devil’s Tower of mashed potatoes in “Close Encounters of the Third Kind.” Garnish with shrimp, lobster tail or grilled marinated steak; the impressive “baconfongo” requires no explanation. Mofongo Tipico stays open later than most restaurants in the area, past midnight on Saturdays and Sundays, when the karaoke scene can get intense.

985 Morris Avenue, facebook.com/mofongoelmocanonyc

Fauzia’s Heavenly Delights

When the air starts to smell like allspice and tamarind, look for the long line and then stand at the back of it. Fauzia’s is a long-running Jamaican cart known as much for its proprietary jerk blend as for nonstandard items like barbecued tofu, excellent fresh vegetables and halal gyro sandwiches. There’s seating across 161st Street in Joyce Kilmer Park, but you’ll need to wait for a day game during the week: Essentially a lunch cart for office workers, Fauzia’s closes around 2:30 p.m. and isn’t open at all on Saturdays and Sundays.

Sidewalk in front of 198 East 161st Street, fauzias.com

Flavas International Grill

The steam tables at Flavas hold a beguiling fusion of Jamaican cuisine and soul food. The jerk chicken is more sauce than smoke, and the sides of choice are mac and cheese, tender butter beans stewed with tomatoes, and mashed yams, sweet with cinnamon. The drinks are of almost equal interest: custom-blended smoothies and juices, along with a traditional peanut punch.

854 Gerard Avenue

Tips for Queens Night Market

By Priya Krishna

By Priya Krishna

Give yourself a break from the restaurant reservations game and head to one of New York City’s many food markets. At the night market in Chinatown or the Bronx, you’ll find dishes and cuisines you probably won’t see in restaurants. The best part? No reservations required. Just a subway ride, some cash and the ability to balance satay and shaved ice in one hand.

I recently rounded up a few veteran marketgoers to explore the Queens Night Market. Here’s what I learned about how to make the most of your experience:

Arrive Early

You may not need a reservation, but you should still plan ahead. Lines at popular stands like Nansense, Burmese Bites and Treat Yourself Jerk Chicken can mean hourlong waits. To beat the crowds, arrive just as the market opens at 5 p.m. You’ll also get more sunlight.

Pick a Rendezvous Point

Twenty minutes spent trying to locate a friend is precious time that could have been spent devouring pupusas. Meet right outside a subway station or at a nearby coffee shop. Dropping a pin can work, but remember: Cell service isn’t always great in crowded places.

Try More Dishes With a Group

Sharing with friends is a fine way to sample a number of dishes in one visit. Three to four people is ideal. Pick a meeting point, then split up. I stood in line at Ay Cachapas! for the gooey, corn-and-cheese-filled cachapas. My friend Max went to Cambodia Now for coconut-laden khmer fish amok. And my friend Tammie went to Brazilicious for a steak sandwich loaded up with toppings. Get in the longest lines first.

Bring Cash

No one likes the person fumbling for bills at the front of the line: I bring a fanny pack, so my money is safely on my person and just a quick zip away. And don’t forget to tip.

Prioritize Portable Foods

I never count on being able to find a table. And I like to let families have first dibs at seating because, well, crying children. So I opt for foods that are easy to eat while walking around: arepas, fritters, skewers, paletas, burgers and the like. There will always be some dishes that are not portable, but simply too tasty not to try. So find a nice patch of grass, secure some spoons and dig in.

Queens Night Market (at the New York Hall of Science), 47-01 111th Street, queensnightmarket.com
Weekend Trips

The Hamptons

By Florence Fabricant

By Florence Fabricant

Just as the farmland still punctuating the Hamptons landscape is retilled every spring, so is much of the restaurant scene. Worth noting is the emergence of serious dining in Hampton Bays, traditionally dotted with casual waterfront spots. Here are destinations both familiar and freshly minted, in the Hamptons and on Long Island’s east end, to satisfy those for whom the word “scene” means a lovely view of boats or gardens.

Good Ground Tavern, Canoe Place Inn & Cottages

Dating from 1697, Canoe Place Inn reopened late last summer after extensive renovation. Its restaurant, beige and tailored indoors, spills into a spacious garden. The menu is mostly Mediterranean fare from the executive chef Ulfet Ozyabasligil Ralph, who is Turkish, and Jerrod Huth, the culinary director, formerly with Thomas Keller. Crusty clams casino and olive oil-poached black bass dressed puttanesca style are standouts.

239 East Montauk Highway, Hampton Bays, canoeplace.com

Crash Cantina

Escape from traffic south of the Montauk Highway at this waterfront Mexican outpost with sunset views. Start with well-made margaritas and chunky, herbaceous guacamole with chips, and sail on from there.

The Inn Spot, 32 Lighthouse Road, Hampton Bays, theinnspot.com

Tutto Caffè

New to East Hampton, in a walkway off Main Street, this stylish, umbrella-shaded addition to the Tutto restaurant group is thoroughly Italian, done in a bronzed Max Mara palette and serving food on Vietri pottery. There are excellent coffees and a full bar, and on the food side, focaccia with mozzarella, tomatoes, arugula and pesto; pinsa Roman pizzas; salads; and pastries. Come for breakfast, lunch, early supper or take away.

55 Main Street, East Hampton, tuttoilgiornocaffe.com

The Clam Bar at Napeague

The very definition of no-frills, this outdoor seafood shack is still a seasonal magnet after more than 40 years. Sweetly briny steamers, oysters or clams on the half shell, steamed lobsters, lobster rolls and fish and chips, often local, are served with friendly efficiency. Cocktails, beer and wine are poured.

2025 Montauk Highway, Amagansett, clambarhamptons.com

Roberta’s Montauk

Roberta’s, which arrived last season from Brooklyn, has returned, again proving that there’s more than pizza in its arsenal. Marinated cucumbers, crispy squid, pastas and yes, pizzas, including a clam pie, are served in an airy whitewashed building with seating indoors and out.

240 Fort Pond Road, Montauk, robertaspizza.com/montauk
Weekend Trips

Kingston, N.Y.

By Nikita Richardson

By Nikita Richardson

For years, Kingston has played understudy to a prima ballerina named Hudson, about 30 miles to the north and across the river. But Kingston’s restaurant scene is just as fun, and slightly closer to New York City — every minute counts, right? Here’s how to make the most of your visit.

The Little Rye Bakehouse

On Saturdays, head to the Kingston farmers’ market and stop by the Little Rye Bakehouse stand, where you can buy flaky puffs filled with grape jelly, and pie by the slice for a midnight snack. If you miss the market, a brick-and-mortar location is open on Sundays. Be warned: A line forms as soon as it opens.

588 Broadway, littlerye.com

Lola

We may have reached peak wood-oven pizza and natural wine, but that doesn’t diminish the joy of eating at Lola, just across the street from the town’s 171-year-old Dutch church. The space is chic, with blond wood and neon-pink accents, and there’s a lovely patio. Pizzas are fired in the main dining room for those who like to watch theirs cook à la minute. That said, a shared plate of freshly made pasta from the menu certainly can’t hurt if pizza isn’t the vibe.

243 Fair Street, lola.pizza

Chleo

Just four months old, Chleo has made its mark as a charming wine bar with a well-considered menu — think campanelle pasta with mushrooms and rich cured egg yolk, or a hefty pork chop on a bed of polenta. And it’s already begun its second act, rebounding from a fire that closed the restaurant for nearly a month. Kingston rallied around them, raising more than $25,000 to support the out-of-work staff. Now there’s a new menu and a comeback to celebrate.

288 Fair Street, chleovin.com

Moonburger

The age of the messy drive-through meatless burger is finally upon us. You can head to Moonburger straightaway if you’d like, but its strategic placement next to the highway also makes it an ideal last bite before hitting the road. The burgers are made with Impossible meat and topped with American cheese (add $1 for a dairy-free cheesy sauce), thinly sliced dill pickles and plenty of onions.

5 Powells Lane, moonburger.com

A Sampling of Cold Noodles in Queens

By Umi Syam

By Umi Syam

A few times a year, I gather friends and lead them on a food crawl in Flushing and Murray Hill, with different themes depending on the season. In summer, it almost always involves cold noodles and a trip to the Koreatown neighborhood that Pete Wells called “the Queens kimchi belt.” You’ll start at the Flushing-Main Street subway stop and end at Broadway-Flushing.

Old Luo Yang

Kick off your tour at the second location of this restaurant, which unlike the first has air-conditioning and seating. Sample anything from the first row of the menu, but the tricolor cold noodle, made of carrot, spinach and purple sweet potato, is a great introduction for first-timers. Pair that with the cumin lamb Chinese burger, and thank yourself for making the trip.

13621 Roosevelt Avenue, oldluoyangtogo.com

Hahm Ji Bach

Walk along Roosevelt Avenue to this Korean hansik restaurant in Murray Hill. Go for the classics, like a soupy mul naengmyeon or a spicy bibim naengmyeon. Or try seolitee kongguksu, a cold black soybean soup. Locals come here for the yeolmu guksu, cold noodles served with young radish water kimchi.

40-11 149th Place

Beat Mazesoba

Make your way north along Northern Boulevard for shared bowls of Japanese mazemen and cold soba. Tuna yukke soba is a light option, but the niku soba may tempt you with its braised Washugyu beef and truffle oil.

161-23 Crocheron Avenue, beatmazesoba.dine.online

Tong Sam Gyup Goo Yi

If you can make it, a proper dinner is just a short walk to a Korean barbecue place famous for its black cast-iron grill plates. By now you should be craving something hot and sizzling for contrast, so pick your protein. But every meal here should end with a high note: kimchi naengmyun served in a bowl made of ice.

162-23 Depot Road, tongsamgyupgooyi.com

A Natural-Wine Stroll in Ridgewood, Queens

By Eric Asimov

By Eric Asimov

On summer weekends, I find a relaxing sense of spaciousness and stillness in the city — especially if the prospect of good food and wine is in the air. In Ridgewood, Queens, young people have filtered in from neighboring Bushwick, Brooklyn, and other areas for the tranquillity of quiet, tree-lined streets of rowhouses. Good restaurants and natural wine have followed.

I suggest going with a small group and sharing bottles and bites, so you don’t fill up too quickly. If you are planning to try a number of different wines, it’s best to get something in the stomach early on.

Decades Pizza

Begin at this restaurant, with its small, bright room and standard-issue gas pizza oven. I immediately spied the pizza chef grating Parmesan onto a pie rather than strewing pre-shredded cheese, a sign that they take ingredients seriously. “The Regular” is essentially an excellent margherita, with creamy mozzarella, spicy tomato, basil and a puffy, blackened crust. Bubbles with pizza are a great combination: Try either a dry, earthy Cinque Campi Lambrusco or a Caneva da Nani Prosecco, both styles that have been demeaned but, when you get past all the bad mass-produced versions, have a lot to offer.

690 Woodward Avenue, decades.pizza

Rolo’s

From there, take a short walk to this popular corner spot on Onderdonk Avenue, great for either a quick snack or a full dinner. Grab corner seats at the pleasant bar. It’s prime time for a palate cleanser, like hamachi crudo, raw slices of amberjack served alongside whatever’s freshest and in season. Try it with a glass of lightly chilled chambourcin, like Early Mountains’ Young Wine, a delightfully refreshing red from Virginia, or, for more bubbles, maybe a half-bottle of St. Donat 5-Bar, a tangy pétillant naturel from Hungary.

853 Onderdonk Avenue, rolosnyc.com

Forêt Wines

Last stop: an intimate natural wine shop around the block, for an after-crawl nightcap. Pick up the Haute Côte de Fruit from Fabien Jouves, or the Beaujolais-Villages from Maison en Belle Lies, two particularly succulent reds.

68-38 Forest Avenue, foretwineshop.com

A Frozen Treats Tour in Downtown Manhattan

By Becky Hughes

By Becky Hughes

To spend summer in New York City is to constantly pingpong between misery and jubilation. All the lows (overheating on the subway platform, spending half of your paycheck on air-conditioning) are quickly countered by twice as many highs (the Philharmonic in the park! Ferry rides! Rockaway Beach! 8 p.m. sunsets!). Perhaps the easiest antidote to a moment of anguish is a sweet, frosty treat. Or, even better, a whole afternoon of them while strolling through downtown Manhattan.

Caffè Panna

Whet your appetite just to the north in Gramercy, at a Roman-style gelateria with surprising daily specials and top-tier sundaes. Best to start the day with a coffee, so try an Aud’s Dream: vanilla sweet cream ice cream with peanut butter swirl, a shot of espresso and whipped cream.

77 Irving Place, caffepanna.com

Ray’s Candy Store

Head southeast to this shop, an Alphabet City mainstay since 1974, for a nostalgic malted chocolate shake. You may even be served by Ray Alvarez himself.

113 Avenue A

Morgenstern’s Bananas

On the Lower East Side, take a brief break from dairy with a swirl of ube cookies and cream soft serve on Rivington Street.

2 Rivington Street, morgensternsbananas.com

Thai Diner

Cap off your day in NoLIta, with a perfect frozen duo: a Bangkok-style beer slushy and a coconut sundae topped with candied peanuts.

186 Mott Street, thaidiner.com

A One-Day Road Trip Through Staten Island

By Sara Bonisteel

By Sara Bonisteel

On a sweltering day, an air-conditioned destination can be summer perfection. This short road trip takes you over the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge for tastes of Albania and Sri Lanka, a stop at a beer garden and an open skate at a throwback rink. Visit them all, or pick and choose to make the day yours.

Blend’s Bakery and Grill

The burek alone, beloved by the A.S.M.R. crowd on TikTok, is worth the drive to this Albanian bakery on a commercial strip in Dongan Hills. But stay for the qebapa, grilled kebabs that come five to a plate with a mess of cabbage and diced cucumbers and tomatoes. Use the pitalka bread, fresh from the oven of this former pizzeria, to make a sandwich.

1718 Hylan Boulevard

Randiwa

The sleepy dining room of this Sri Lankan restaurant, in a nondescript shopping plaza in Bulls Head, masks the pleasures that await. Here you’ll find lamprie — rice, cashews, caramelized eggplant, onion chutney, a deep-fried egg and a fish ball cloaked in banana leaves, along with the meat of your choice. If the lacy bowl-shaped rice flour and coconut milk pancakes known as hoppers are not available, try the string hoppers instead, rice noodle patties served with a spicy coconut sambal and kiri hodi, a thin coconut milk gravy. Wash it down with a lime juice, a whole lime blended peel and all, sweetened and served over ice.

1405 Richmond Avenue, randiwa.net

Killmeyer’s Old Bavaria Inn

Arthur Kill Road loops past auto body shops as it cuts through the greenery of the Clay Pit Ponds State Park Preserve. When the Merry Wanderer, an eight-foot Hummel figurine, appears around the bend, you’ll know you’ve arrived. The Wanderer, said to be the world’s largest Hummel, may be the least interesting thing about Killmeyer’s. In its more than 175-year history, this restaurant has been a town store, inn and watering hole. For the last two decades it has tapped into Black Forest charm, with steins of beer served on a hand-carved mahogany bar, and a beer garden that hosts live music.

4254 Arthur Kill Road, killmeyers.com

RollerJam USA

In Tottenville, with the Outerbridge Crossing in the distance, you’ll find a roller rink that has been serving the island since 2007. Friday nights and Saturday afternoons are all-ages open skates; it’s 21 and up on Saturday nights. The D.J. shuffles through Childish Gambino, Cardi B and Taylor Swift as skaters sway under the black lights. Skee-Ball and $2 plain slices feed the soul. When you leave, there is a Sonic serving cherry limeade over pebble ice at the end of the block.

236 Richmond Valley Road, rollerjamusa.com
Food Crawls

An Afternoon of Delights in Jersey City, N.J.

By Alexa Weibel

By Alexa Weibel

Though Jersey City has long been a destination for pizza, with Bread and Salt and Razza providing culinary clout, its restaurants serve — and reflect — a diverse population. Few places seem to demonstrate this idea as well as a seven-block stretch of restaurants on Newark Avenue. Plan for an afternoon venture to maximize your options.

Philippine Bread House

Start at this 44-year-old neighborhood fixture for dozens of freshly baked Filipino treats that blur the line between salty and sweet. With $20, you can fill a bag with best-selling petite ensaymadas, sapin-sapin (coconut and rice flour cakes), empanadas and mango pianono (roll cake).

530 Newark Avenue, philippinebreadhouse.com

Freetown Road Project

Visit this Caribbean restaurant focusing on the food of Antigua and Barbuda for glazed, breaded BBQ jerk wings with a lingering whisper of allspice. Curry chicken roti comes with golden flatbread the size of a record sleeve, just sturdy enough for scooping.

640 Newark Avenue, freetownroadprojectnj.org

Mezcal Kitchen

Saunter over for happy-hour margaritas and agua frescas (Jamaica, cucumber-lemon and tamarind) to pair with cilantro-lime fish ceviche tostadas or hearty masa-and-pinto bean huaraches, kneaded in house.

665 Newark Avenue, mezcalkitchenjc.com

Korai Kitchen

Call in a takeout order to save for tomorrow from this Bangladeshi spot, which aims to resume dine-in service later this summer. Try the curries, including Rohu fish imported from Bangladesh, plus their sunny, turmeric-stained shrimp dopiaza, glistening with tomatoes and molten onions.

576 Summit Avenue, koraikitchen.com

Honest

Finally, beeline to India Square for chaat, including bite-size orbs of pani puri, Indian Chinese specialties like paneer chile and an extensive South Indian dosa menu. Cooked on one side until crisp, and spongy on the other, the lacy rava masala dosa is a tangy, textural delight built to tear and share.

811 Newark Avenue, honestrestaurantsusa.com
